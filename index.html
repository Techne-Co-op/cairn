Cairn
A commons for making sense of the web, together.
Peers share what is worth attention, and stack their judgment in the open. What a thing is, and whether it can be trusted, is built by the many rather than decided by the few.
an introduction · a concept with a working prototype no prior knowledge assumed
01 · The absence

There is nowhere to make sense of the web in common.

Every day, billions of links pass between people. We send each other articles, videos, papers, and the strange new artifacts that machines now produce. And almost nowhere does this passing-along gather into something a community can see and trust.

Links move through feeds built to hold attention rather than to reward what deserves it. They move through private messages that vanish, and through platforms that decide on our behalf what rises and what sinks. What a thing is, whether it is worth your time, whether it is even true, tends to be one of three things: left unsaid, imposed from above by a platform's ranking, or quietly gamed by whoever has the most to gain. None of these is a commons. None of them belongs to the people doing the reading.

This absence grows more serious as machines learn to generate endless plausible material. When anything can be produced, the scarce and valuable act is no longer making content but judging it: deciding, together and in the open, what merits attention and what a thing actually is. That judgment is exactly what the current web has no shared, trustworthy, commonly owned place to do. Cairn is a proposal for that place.

02 · The gesture

Stones, stacked by many hands.

On a mountain trail, where no path is marked, hikers leave a small pile of stones to show the way. It is called a cairn. No one owns it. No one edits it. Each stone is one person, having walked the ground, quietly adding their agreement that this way matters. The next walker reads the height of the pile and trusts the many who came before.

Cairn brings that gesture to the web. A peer shares a link. Other peers who find it worth attention add a stone. The pile grows, or it does not, and its height is a signal no single voice could fake: the accumulated judgment of many hands. Alongside the stones, peers lay #tags, the trails that show where collective attention is moving, so that a reader can follow not just what is high but what is rising, and along which line of interest.

So far this is simple, and on purpose. The depth of Cairn is in two turns it takes from here. The first concerns who decides what a thing means. The second concerns what a stone actually is.

02 · The first turn

Meaning is built by peers, not stuck on.

On most platforms a tag is a label. Someone types #tech-leak and it sits there, a flat fact, the same whether one person or ten thousand agreed. Cairn treats a tag as something larger: a claim a peer makes and stands behind, which the rest of the network is then free to confirm, sharpen, qualify, or contest.

So when a peer asserts that a link is a tech leak, others who encounter it can agree, adding their weight. They can refine, proposing that it is more precisely a supply-chain leak. They can qualify, noting that it sits behind a paywall. Or they can contest, arguing that it is only a rumor. Each of these moves carries weight, and the distribution of that weight across the competing claims is the network's judgment. A reader arriving at the link does not see a single imposed label. They see a living field of where the community has reached agreement and where it is still arguing.

Two consequences matter. Because nothing is deleted, a minority reading survives in the record, so that a confident crowd which turns out to be wrong can be corrected later by the dissent it once overruled. And because the field is always open, the meaning of a link is never finished: if a story is later debunked, fresh weight can move the judgment, and the confidence that was high will fall. The metadata is alive. Over many links and much time, this is how a shared vocabulary grows: not designed by a committee, but precipitated from use, the words the community has actually invested its judgment in rising to become the language everyone shares.

02 · The second turn

The unit of judgment is attention, not money.

If peers are placing stones, and stones carry weight, it is fair to ask what a stone is. The easy answer would be a tiny coin, a fraction of a cent. Cairn takes a more careful path, and borrows it from an old and well-understood instrument: the postage stamp.

A stamp is strange, if you look closely. It is not money. You cannot spend it at the grocer, and its worth does not rise and fall with markets. A stamp is a prepaid promise of a service: one letter carried. A forever stamp mails one letter today and one letter a decade from now, no matter what the price of postage has done in between, because its value was never a price. Its value is the work it commands. Stable value comes from stable utility.

A stone in Cairn is a stamp of this kind. It is one unit of the commons' real work, which for a place built to make sense of the web is attention: a unit of evaluation rendered, a measure of judgment given. You earn stones by doing that work, by sharing and assessing and curating, and you spend them by drawing on the commons' attention in turn. The supply is not minted from nothing and it is not bought with wealth; it is mutual credit, issued between peers and held in common, the way neighbors have kept reciprocal accounts for centuries. Its worth is anchored not to a dollar but to a service the commons can actually perform, which means it neither inflates nor invites speculation.

Why this matters

When meaning is built by peers, the question becomes what currency that building runs on. If it runs on money, the wealthy name the world. If it runs on attention, the engaged name it, and the cost of an opinion is a unit of one's own care rather than one's bank balance. Pricing judgment in attention rather than dollars is what keeps a sensemaking commons a commons.

03 · What holds it together

Care travels with it, and the rules are in plain sight.

A commons without tending decays, so Cairn carries care as a first-class act. Peers can flag what should be handled differently, and the response is graduated: a first concern covers a sensitive link, a stronger one lowers it, and only the heaviest removes it. Sensitive material is hidden by default, so that younger readers walk only marked trails. Crucially, none of this is decided by a hidden algorithm or a distant moderator. The thresholds that govern it are published where they apply, and they belong to the community to change. This follows a long line of research, most associated with Elinor Ostrom, into how real commons govern themselves: clear boundaries, rules the participants can see, and sanctions that escalate by degree rather than fall all at once.

The same commitment to legibility runs through everything. There is no personalized feed: every reader with the same filters sees the same field, so a classroom can look at one screen together and a community can argue about a shared picture rather than ten thousand private ones. And there is no machinery of capture: no infinite scroll, no streaks, no notifications pulling you back. A good visit to Cairn is a short one. You arrive, you find what is worth your time, and you leave through it to read. Leaving is the point.

04 · A lineage, not a novelty

Old ideas, newly joined.

None of the parts of Cairn are invented from nothing. Its interest is in the joining, and it stands in a real tradition of people who believed tools could help us think together rather than merely consume.

Vannevar Bush
In 1945 imagined the memex, a machine for building and sharing trails of association through knowledge. Cairn's tags are those trails, laid in common.
Douglas Engelbart
Spent his life on the augmentation of human intellect: tools that raise a group's collective capability, not ones that replace its thinking. A commons for shared judgment is squarely in that aim.
The postal service
Showed that reliable, universal, affordable communication can be infrastructure rather than a product, and that a prepaid stamp can hold stable value by promising a service rather than a price.
Elinor Ostrom
Demonstrated that commons are not doomed to ruin; communities govern shared resources well when the rules are visible, the boundaries clear, and the sanctions graduated.
Mutual credit
A centuries-old practice, from local exchange networks to contemporary cooperatives, in which credit is issued between members rather than borrowed from a bank, and value is a record of mutual recognition rather than a scarce coin.

Cairn grows from a working cooperative rather than a startup, which is why these ideas are load-bearing in it rather than decorative. The commons is not a marketing frame; it is the actual structure, and the stone economy is an extension of how that cooperative already accounts for the contributions of its members.

05 · What it could become

Not one place, but many, connected.

The deepest reason to call Cairn a platform rather than a website is that it is not meant to be a single destination owned by anyone. A school, a watershed group, a field of researchers, a neighborhood, each could run its own Cairn: its own commons, its own vocabulary, its own stones earned and spent locally. What joins them is not a central server and not a shared global coin, but the same arrangement the postal services have used for over a century to carry a letter across borders without a world currency: published terms, and bilateral clearing between independent members. A unit of judgment earned in one commons can be honored in another by agreement, node to node.

What this could amount to, in time, is a piece of civic infrastructure for collective intelligence: a way for communities to make sense of the world together, owning that work in common, with no platform standing between them and harvesting the attention they spend. That is the theory Cairn exists to test.

06 · Where it stands

An honest account of what this is.

Cairn is, today, a concept with a working prototype. The gesture is real and you can touch it: stones, tags, the live field, the graduated care. The economy described here, stones as a service credit pinned to attention, and the conviction field in which peers build meaning, are a worked design under active review, not a launched product. Every number in the model is a proposal awaiting the judgment of the people who will govern it, and the parts of the design that touch law are being reviewed by counsel before anything is built on them.

This is not a weakness of the introduction; it is the introduction being honest, which is the same honesty the platform itself is built to keep. Throughout Cairn, small marks name the condition of things, always a word beside the color, never the color alone, so that reading the system is reading its candor.

If the idea moves you, the right response is not to sign up for anything. It is to think along: to ask whether a commons for judgment, run on attention and held in common, is a thing the web is missing, and whether it could be made to work. That question is the invitation.
